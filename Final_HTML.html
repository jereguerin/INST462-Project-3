The Young Who Didn't Make It

They said COVID-19 would spare the young, but for thousands of Americans between 18 and 34, it never did.

By Jeremy Guerin  ·  Based on CDC National Vital Statistics System data  ·  Published May 8, 2026

In January 2020, as the COVID-19 Pandemic kicked off, public health officials focused their efforts on protecting the elderly. The public was assured that young adults (18–34) faced little risk of death. And for the most part, this was true, as the elderly constituted the vast majority of COVID-19-related deaths.

But although death rates were low, more than 12,600 young adults died from COVID-19 in the United States between 2019 and 2024 according to CDC records, and in some areas of the country, this rate was alarmingly higher than others. Families lost children, siblings, partners, and parents in spite of the public messaging suggesting that these people would survive.

Deaths from COVID-19 among young adults were disproportionately overrepresented in states with high poverty rates, poor health infrastructure, and larger populations of racial minorities. Despite COVID-19 only killing young adults at a crude rate of roughly 2.0 per 100,000 people nationwide, two states, New Mexico and Mississippi, faced a rate nearly triple the national average.

The Epicenter: New Mexico

No other state saw a higher rate of young adult deaths from COVID-19 than New Mexico. New Mexico faced a crude death rate of 6.2 per 100,000 young adults, more than three times the national average, setting itself apart from every other state in the country. Among a young adult population of just over 3.3 million, a total of 208 young adults between 18 and 34 died from COVID-19 there.

Second in Sorrow: Mississippi

Mississippi ranked second in terms of young adult deaths from COVID-19 in the United States. With a crude rate of 5.0 per 100,000 and 232 total deaths, a rate more than twice the national average, and representative of a profound failure of the state's healthcare system in one of America's most impoverished states.

New Mexico and Mississippi were not mere anomalies, but a result of decades of underinvestment in healthcare, high poverty rates, and a history of racial discrimination that left young people of color particularly vulnerable.

The Poverty Trap

In impoverished communities, young adults were particularly vulnerable to COVID-19. Poverty correlates with poor health outcomes, with impoverished Americans having faced limited access to preventive care, higher rates of underlying conditions, overcrowded living situations, and jobs that failed to offer remote work or sick leave during the COVID-19 Pandemic.

New Mexico and Mississippi are two of the poorest states in the nation by family poverty rate, with Mississippi leading all 50 states at a rate of 14.3% of families living below the poverty line. New Mexico, ranking 49th nationally, trails just behind at 13.7%. The national average across all 50 states is 8.7%.

As a result of Mississippi and New Mexico's poverty rates, COVID-19 ravaged not just the old but even the young. Fewer hospital beds, longer distances to hospitals in rural communities, and higher rates of the underlying conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, and obesity due to lesser access to healthy foods, made many young adults vulnerable to complications from COVID-19.

Race, Risk, and a Preventable Toll

But perhaps the most concerning aspect of young adult deaths from COVID-19 was not just how they were disproportionately represented among the poor, but how they were disproportionately represented among racial minorities. Racial minorities — a category encompassing American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian, Black or African American, Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander, and multiracial individuals — died from COVID-19 at significantly higher rates than their white counterparts in almost every state.

On a national level, non-white young adults died at a crude rate of 2.9 per 100,000, compared to 2.2 per 100,000 for white young adults. But the racial gap grew dramatically in states with the highest overall deaths. New Mexico saw a minority death rate among young adults reach a staggering 12.9 per 100,000 — more than three times the white rate of 4.3. The majority of these deaths consisted of Native Americans, America's most impoverished ethnic group. Mississippi didn't fare much better at 6.8 per 100,000, with most of these deaths consisting of Black people, compared to 3.4 for white young adults.

Systemic racism in healthcare made the COVID-19 Pandemic much more dangerous for minority communities in New Mexico and Mississippi. Racial minorities in the United States are often residentially segregated in areas with less access to healthcare and are overrepresented in front-line roles considered essential with little opportunity to socially distance. Consequently, the Pandemic was yet another chapter in America's history of discrimination.

Whether or not your state's minority-to-white death ratio mirrors the national rate or exceeds it, the loss of life that occurred as a result of this disparity could have been easily mended with more equitable access to healthcare and housing, and better working conditions.

A Failure We Must Not Repeat

The story of COVID-19 deaths among young adults in the United States is a story of two pandemics happening simultaneously; among America's middle and upper class, it was a story of social distancing, school shutdowns, and remote work. But for impoverished and minority communities in states like New Mexico and Mississippi, many of the opportunities to practice social distancing were absent, and health inequality allowed the virus to target even the young despite popular health authorities insisting they would be spared. Should America allow this injustice to continue, more young adults in these communities will perish in the next pandemic.
